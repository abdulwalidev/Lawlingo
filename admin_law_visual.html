📚 Topic map — what this is all about
The big picture
⚖️ 1. Procedural exclusivity — O'Reilly v Mackman [1983]
The courtroom drama (stick figure edition)
THE RULE (O'Reilly): If you have a PUBLIC LAW claim → you MUST use AJR (Ord 53 / CPR Pt 54). Using ordinary action = abuse of process.
Ordinary action vs AJR — why it matters
⚠ Ordinary Action (BAD for public law)
Bypasses 3-month time limit
Avoids safeguards for public bodies
Keeps bodies in uncertainty longer
= ABUSE OF PROCESS per O'Reilly
✓ AJR / Judicial Review (correct)
3-month time limit (certainty)
Requires permission stage
Protects public administration
Cross-exam limited but available
3 exceptions — when ordinary action IS allowed
🔑 Must-know cases — public/private divide
Datafin [1987] — Takeover Panel
MUST KNOW
Panel = private but woven into governmental regulatory framework → amenable to JR. Test: nature of function, not just source of power.
Exam: Use to argue a body IS susceptible to JR despite private/contractual origins.
Aga Khan [1993] — Jockey Club
NOT amenable to JR — not woven into system of GOVERNMENTAL control. Tightened Datafin: must show "governmental" not just "public" function.
Exam: Contrast with Datafin. Shows the limits of amenability.
Wandsworth v Winder [1984]
Tenant defending possession action could raise ultra vires rent increase as defence. Defence = matter of right; no abuse of process. Private contractual right dominated.
Roy v Kensington FPC [1992]
GP had bundle of private law rights → ordinary action allowed even though public law elements present. Private rights "dominating" proceedings.
Cocks v Thanet [1983]
Housing Act: public law duty (inquiry) was condition precedent to private right → must use Ord 53. Contrast with Winder: here private right didn't yet EXIST.
Mercury Communications [1996]
Substance was a contractual dispute → flexibility retained. Not abuse to use ordinary action where Ord 53 not peculiarly suited.
🚪 2. Ouster clauses — Parliament trying to slam the door on courts
What is an ouster clause? (stick figure scene)
OUSTER CLAUSE = statutory provision attempting to exclude/restrict courts from reviewing administrative decisions.
Core tension: Parliamentary sovereignty (obey Parliament) vs Rule of law (courts must keep JR).
Chronological evolution of ouster clause law
56
Smith v East Elloe RDC [1956]
Courts DEFERRED — wrong approach
6-week limit upheld even against bad faith allegation. Courts reluctant. Now largely discredited.
★
Anisminic v Foreign Compensation Commission [1969]
⭐ THE BIG ONE — ouster clauses broken
FCC made legal error → decision = NULLITY = not a "determination" → ouster clause didn't apply. Abolished distinction between errors going to jurisdiction and errors within jurisdiction.
MUST KNOW
77
R v SSE ex p Ostler [1977]
Partial ouster (time limits) upheld
6-week limit in planning cases = valid partial ouster. Courts accept time limits as legitimate.
★
R (Privacy International) v IPT [2019] UKSC
⭐ Anisminic reaffirmed + extended
Lord Carnwath: JR can only be excluded by "most clear and explicit words." Parliament must explicitly confront exclusion. Vague formulas insufficient. Rule of law requires judicial oversight.
MUST KNOW
23
R (Oceana) v Upper Tribunal [2023]
s.11A TCFA 2007 — explicit partial ouster works
Saini J: where Parliament clearly addresses exclusion → courts must give effect. Exceptions: bad faith, no original jurisdiction, fundamental breach of natural justice.
Anisminic — before vs after (jurisdiction diagram)
Full ouster vs partial ouster — know the difference
Full ouster — courts very suspicious
Tries to exclude JR completely
Needs "most clear and explicit words" (Privacy International)
Anisminic: error = nullity = clause doesn't bite
Almost always ineffective post-Anisminic
Partial ouster — generally accepted
Time limits (e.g. 6 weeks in planning)
Restricts WHO can claim
Ostler [1977]: time limits = valid
s.11A TCFA 2007: Upper Tribunal Cart claims restricted
Lord Reid's list — what makes a decision a NULLITY (Anisminic)
Even if tribunal had jurisdiction to start inquiry, decision = nullity if it:
1. Was made in bad faith
2. Made a decision it had no power to make
3. Failed to comply with natural justice
4. Misconstrued provisions → decided the wrong question
5. Refused to consider what it was required to consider
6. Based decision on matter it had no right to take into account
Lord Reid: "I do not intend this list to be exhaustive."
🔍 3. Availability of JR — jurisdiction, amenability, justiciability
Three gate questions before JR succeeds
Errors of law — the Anisminic revolution
Pre-Anisminic: only errors "going to" jurisdiction reviewed
→
Post-Anisminic: ALL errors of law = ultra vires = reviewable
Lord Diplock in Re Racal [1981]: "The old distinction was for practical purposes abolished."
Errors of fact — 3 grounds (less reviewable than law)
1
Jurisdictional / precedent fact
Fact determines WHETHER body has jurisdiction → court reviews it. Is this person a "child"? → objective question, right/wrong answer = court decides. R(A) v Croydon [2009].
2
Law/fact distinction — objective vs subjective
Is child "in need"? (Subjective/evaluative) → left to authority. Is person a "child"? (Objective) → court decides. Baroness Hale in R(A) v Croydon.
3
Material error of fact — E v SSHD [2004]
4 requirements: (i) mistake as to existing fact; (ii) fact was established/uncontentious; (iii) claimant not responsible; (iv) mistake material to reasoning. Carnwath LJ.
MUST KNOW — 4 requirements
Justiciability — when courts REFUSE to decide
🧠 4. Theories of judicial review (Topic 2)
Two competing theories
Ultra vires theory
JR authorised by Parliament (express or implied)
Constitutionally orthodox — supports sovereignty
Parliament could theoretically abolish JR
Modified: Parliament impliedly intends rule of law
Forsyth & Elliott — legitimacy of JR (2003)
Common law theory
JR = inherent jurisdiction of High Court
Grounds fashioned by common law, not Parliament
Strong version: courts could strike down primary legislation
Weak version: accepts parliamentary sovereignty
Jowell 1999; Allan 2002
Allan's critique: Both theories agree on more than they differ — debate is "futile." Focus should shift to defining rule of law content in our constitution.
Red light vs green light (Harlow & Rawlings)
🔴 Red light
Suspicious of state power
Courts as protector of individual
Judicial control preferred
Links to Diceyan orthodoxy
🟢 Green light
Less suspicious of state
Facilitate good administration
Prefer political accountability
Courts as obstruction to effectiveness
